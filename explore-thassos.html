Thassos is the greenest island in the Aegean — pine forests run right down to the sea, marble glows white under the water, and the whole island is only about 40 km across, so nothing is ever far. The locals call it the emerald isle, and from late spring to autumn it stays lush in a way the dry Cycladic islands never do.

Casa Valesa sits in Skala Sotiros on the quiet west coast — the side of the island most visitors drive past. This is the calm, local, low-key Thassos: small fishing harbours, seafront fish tavernas, olive groves climbing the hills, and sunsets straight into the sea. You trade the big resort beaches of the east for peace, real village life, and a base that's genuinely central. From here the famous spots (Golden Beach, Giola, Marble Beach, the old capital Theologos, the archaeology of Limenas) are all an easy day trip, while your own stretch of coast stays uncrowded.

This guide is written from a Skala Sotiros base — every drive time below is measured from your front door. Use it to plan lazy beach days, mountain-village wanders, archaeology mornings, boat afternoons and slow seafood dinners.

🏖️ Beaches & secret coves

Thassos beaches split into two worlds: the long, busy, sandy beaches of the east and south coasts (Golden Beach, Paradise, Psili Ammos) and the wilder, quieter coves of the north and west. The water everywhere is famously clear — Thassian marble on the seabed turns it turquoise. The west coast around Skala Sotiros is the quiet side: small pebble-and-sand coves, fewer crowds, and the island's best sunsets. Bring water shoes for the pebble beaches and reef-edged coves.

Golden Beach (Chrysi Ammoudia / Skala Potamia)

~35–40 min · east coast · long soft sand

The island's most famous beach — a long sweep of fine golden sand in a green bay backed by pine-covered hills, with shallow, gently shelving water. The northern end (Skala Potamia) is more developed with tavernas, sunbeds and water sports; the southern end (Chrysi Akti) is a touch quieter. The headline family beach on Thassos.

Local tip: Park at the southern Chrysi Akti end for an easier walk to the water and a quieter patch of sand; go for a long lunch at a seafront taverna and stay for the afternoon.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Saliara / Marble Beach (Marble Beach)

~50–60 min (incl. rough track) · north coast · white marble pebbles

A small, dazzling cove where the 'sand' is crushed white marble and the sea glows an unreal milky turquoise — one of the most photographed spots on the island. It's beautiful but small and gets busy, with a beach bar in season.

Local tip: ⚠️ The final access road is a rough, unpaved, potholed track — a normal hire car can do it slowly and carefully but a small low car or a wet day make it harder; many people take an organised jeep/4x4 trip instead. Go early to get a spot and beat both the crowds and the midday glare.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Giola (natural rock lagoon)

~55–65 min · near Astris (south) · rock pool, no sand

A spectacular natural rock pool carved into the cliffs, filled with deep clear seawater and connected to the open sea — like a private lagoon in the rocks. Not a beach at all: you walk in over rock and climb/jump down into the pool. Adventurous swimmers love it; cliff-jumping happens here but is at your own risk.

Local tip: Go EARLY (before ~10:00) or near sunset — midday it's crowded and the sun is harsh with no shade. Park at the top and walk down ~10–15 min over a rocky path; wear proper shoes, not flip-flops, and bring your own water. Not suitable for small children or weak swimmers.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Aliki (Paradise side) & Aliki cove

~45–55 min · south-east · sand-and-pebble twin coves

One of the loveliest spots on Thassos: a slim peninsula with a cove on each side and the ancient marble quarry and double-basilica ruins on the headland above. Crystal water, a handful of tavernas, an archaeological feel. Combines a swim with real history.

Local tip: Swim on the more sheltered side depending on the wind, then walk the headland to the half-submerged ancient quarry and the early-Christian basilica ruins. Snorkellers can spot worked marble blocks under the water.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Paradise Beach (Makryammos Kinira side / Paradise)

~45–55 min · east/south-east · golden sand

A long, beautiful golden-sand beach with turquoise water, backed by greenery — one of the most scenic on the island, with a lively beach-bar scene at one end and quieter sand if you walk along. Popular with a younger crowd at the bar end.

Local tip: Walk away from the main beach bar for a calmer stretch of sand. Good for a full beach day with food and drinks on hand.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Psili Ammos

~45–55 min · south-east (near Kinira) · fine sand

'Fine sand' is exactly what you get — a small, sheltered, shallow sandy bay that's excellent for families and young children, with tavernas and sunbeds. Calm, warm, easy.

Local tip: One of the best family beaches on the island thanks to the shallow, gentle water — pair it with nearby Kinira.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Trypiti

~30–40 min · south coast · sand & pebble

A relaxed south-coast beach near Potos with light sand, clear water and a famous offshore rock formation with a natural 'hole' (trypiti = pierced) you can swim out to. Organised with sunbeds and tavernas but easygoing.

Local tip: Strong swimmers can swim out to the pierced rock; otherwise just enjoy an easy, well-serviced beach close to Potos for lunch.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Astris (Astrida)

~50–60 min · south coast · sand & pebble

A pretty south-coast bay near the Giola turn-off, calmer and less hectic than the big east-coast names, with clear water, sunbeds and a couple of tavernas. A good base for the Giola + Aliki south loop.

Local tip: Combine with Giola (just up the road) and a south-coast taverna lunch.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Notos / Salonikios beach

~55–65 min · south-east tip (Kinira/Notos area) · sand & pebble, semi-wild

A quieter, more natural beach on the south-eastern side — less organised, more relaxed, with the small islet of Kinira offshore. The reward for driving a bit further: space and calm even in August.

Local tip: Bring your own water and shade if you want the wild-beach feel; for facilities stay closer to Kinira.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Quiet west-coast coves (Skala Sotiros ↔ Skala Kallirachis)

~0–15 min · west coast · pebble & sand, mostly unorganised

This is your home stretch — the calm, shallow, west-facing coast right on Casa Valesa's doorstep. Skala Sotiros itself has a simple, friendly local beach with tavernas a few steps from the water. Heading south toward Skala Prinos, Skala Marion and Skala Kallirachis you'll find a string of small, mostly pebble coves, fishing-harbour beaches and unmarked pull-offs that stay quiet even in high season. Calm water (the west is sheltered) and the island's best sunsets straight into the sea.

Local tip: Pack a cool-bag, walk or drive 5–10 minutes either side of the harbour, and pick whichever empty cove takes your eye. Water shoes help on the pebbles. Stay for sunset and then dinner at a Skala Sotiros or Skala Kallirachis seafront taverna — that's the whole west-coast secret.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

🏘️ Villages to explore

Thassos has two layers of villages: the coastal skales (little ports — Skala Sotiros, Skala Prinos, Skala Kallirachis…) and the older upper villages built inland and uphill, away from the historic pirate raids. The upper villages — Theologos, Panagia, Sotiras, Kastro, Maries — are where the real, traditional Thassos lives: stone houses, slate roofs, plane-shaded squares, spring water and mountain tavernas. A village afternoon is one of the best things you can do on the island.

Sotiras (Sotiros) — your mountain village

~10–15 min uphill · west · sunset taverna views

The traditional stone village directly above Skala Sotiros — your closest upper village. A handful of old houses, a plane-tree square and tavernas perched with wide-open views west over the coast and out to sea. Sotiras has an old mining history (the area was worked in the 20th-century mining era), and the village taverna terraces are one of the island's classic sunset spots.

Local tip: Drive up for an early-evening drink or dinner and time it for sunset over the sea — it's a 10-minute trip from Casa Valesa and an easy, repeatable favourite. Order grilled meat and the local wine.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Theologos — the old capital

~30–40 min · south interior · historic & traditional

The medieval/Ottoman-era capital of Thassos and the island's best-preserved traditional village: a long single street of whitewashed stone houses with slate roofs, schist walls and tall cypresses, set in a green valley. Wonderfully atmospheric, protected as a traditional settlement. Famous for its spit-roast lamb and goat (kid) tavernas — many slow-roast the meat for hours.

Local tip: Come for a long lunch or dinner and eat the spit-roasted goat/lamb (kokkinisto / souvla) it's known for. Wander the whole street, see the old folklore museum and stone fountains, and pair it with nearby Kastro or Maries.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Panagia — the mountain spring village

~40–50 min · north-east interior · springs, plane trees, lamb

A beautiful, lively mountain village just above Golden Beach: steep cobbled lanes, stone-and-slate houses with wooden balconies, flowing spring fountains and huge plane trees shading the square. One of the most picturesque villages on the island and a great place to eat traditional food — grilled lamb, goat, and local sweets.

Local tip: Park at the edge and walk up to the main square with the spring and plane trees; have lunch at a taverna there, then drop down to Golden Beach for the afternoon. Try the local honey and walnut sweets.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Potos — the lively south resort

~30–35 min · south coast · resort & nightlife

The main resort of the south coast: a long sandy beach, a pedestrian seafront packed with tavernas, cafés, bars and shops. Much busier and more touristy than the west coast — the place to go when you want buzz, choice of restaurants, evening strolls and a drink by the sea.

Local tip: Good for an evening out and a change of pace from the quiet west — eat at the harbour end, then walk the seafront. Theologos is just up the road for a contrast.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Limenaria — the south town

~25–30 min · south-west coast · town with mining heritage

The second-largest town on Thassos and the largest on the south side, with a real working-town feel: a long waterfront, a marina, shops, banks and tavernas, plus the striking old Palataki ('little palace') — the former Speidel/Vieille Montagne mining-company mansion above the harbour, a relic of the early-20th-century mining era. A good base for south-coast services and a pleasant seafront.

Local tip: Walk up to the Palataki for the view and the mining history, then have coffee or dinner along the waterfront. Closest 'proper town' to the west-coast base for shopping.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

The Prinos villages (Mikros/Megalos Prinos, Skala Prinos, Kallirachi & Skala Kallirachis)

~5–20 min · west coast · ferry port + olive country

Your immediate neighbours down the west coast. Skala Prinos is the island's secondary ferry port (boats to Kavala) with a harbour, tavernas and a workaday feel. Inland, Mikros and Megalos Prinos (Kazaviti) are lovely old upper villages tucked into the hills, full of stone houses, plane trees and springs — wonderfully green and traditional. Further south, Kallirachi is a quiet hillside village above its little port Skala Kallirachis, which has some of the best low-key seafront fish tavernas on the island. This is olive-and-wine country.

Local tip: Drive up to Megalos/Mikros Prinos (Kazaviti) for the green old-village atmosphere and a shaded coffee, then come back down for a fresh-fish dinner at Skala Kallirachis harbour — a perfect easy evening from Casa Valesa.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Maries

~30–40 min · west interior · quiet mountain village, water & nature

A small, peaceful inland village in the wooded hills of the west side, near reservoirs and streams — a base for walkers and for the waterfall/nature walks of the Kastro–Maries area. Genuinely off the tourist track, green and quiet, with a traditional square.

Local tip: Use it as a starting point for nature walks and the dam/lake area; combine with Theologos and Kastro on a mountain loop.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Kastro

~50–60 min (last stretch rough) · high interior · abandoned/semi-abandoned mountain refuge

The highest and most remote old village on Thassos — a former refuge built deep in the mountains to hide from pirates, largely abandoned now but with a handful of stone houses, a church and a single seasonal taverna. Reached by a long, partly rough mountain road. The reward is silence, big mountain views and a real sense of old Thassos.

Local tip: Check the road conditions and your car before going; the final section is unpaved. Best as part of a mountain day trip via Theologos/Maries — and bring water.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Limenas (Thassos Town) — the capital

~30–35 min · north coast · capital, port, archaeology

The island's capital and main port (ferries to Keramoti), and the cultural heart of Thassos. A lively harbour ringed with cafés and tavernas, the old fishing port (Palio Limani), good shopping — and, woven right through the town, the ancient city: the Agora, the Archaeological Museum, the city walls and gates, and the path up to the ancient theatre and acropolis. Town and antiquity in one place.

Local tip: Combine a morning of archaeology (Agora + Museum + walk up to the ancient theatre) with lunch at the picturesque old harbour, then coffee by the marina. The best single day for history-plus-town life.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

🏛️ Archaeological & historical sites

Thassos was a wealthy ancient city-state, and the antiquities are remarkable and uncrowded — most of them concentrated in and around the capital, Limenas, where the modern town sits on top of the ancient one. Entry to the main archaeological area and museum is typically a small fee; opening hours vary by season, so check locally. Many of the ruins (walls, gates, sanctuaries) are open and free to wander.

Ancient Agora of Thassos (Limenas)

~30–35 min · Limenas town centre

The civic and commercial heart of the ancient city, right beside the old harbour: a large excavated square of stoas, foundations, altars and monuments from the Classical through Roman periods. You walk among the columns and bases where the ancient Thasians did business and politics. The anchor site of the island's archaeology.

Local tip: Start here, then walk straight into the Archaeological Museum next to it — they make sense together. Go in the morning before the heat.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Ancient Theatre & Acropolis of Thassos

~30–35 min · hillside above Limenas (short uphill walk)

A beautiful ancient theatre set into the pine-covered hillside above the town, with the sea as its backdrop (it sometimes hosts summer performances). Above and beyond it climbs the acropolis, with the remains of sanctuaries (Athena, Pan), Byzantine fortifications and medieval Genoese walls, and sweeping views. The walk up links the theatre, the acropolis and the famous 'Secret Stairway' rock-cut steps back down.

Local tip: Wear proper shoes and bring water — it's an uphill walk on rock. The reverse loop down the rock-cut Secret Stairway to the Gate of Parmenon is a highlight; do it in the cooler hours.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

City walls & ancient gates

~30–35 min · ringing Limenas

The ancient city was encircled by impressive marble fortification walls, and several monumental gates survive, carved with reliefs of gods and mythical figures — among them the Gate of Silenus, the Gate of Parmenon (still with its lintel) and others dedicated to Hermes, the Graces, Zeus and Hera. Following the wall circuit is like tracing the outline of the whole ancient city, much of it free to walk.

Local tip: Pick up a site map at the museum/Agora and follow the wall to spot the carved gates — the Gate of Silenus reliefs are a favourite. A great self-guided walk.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Archaeological Museum of Thassos

~30–35 min · next to the Agora, Limenas

A well-curated museum holding the island's finest finds: the colossal Archaic kouros carrying a ram, marble sculpture, pottery, coins, inscriptions and grave goods spanning the city's history. The best single place to understand how rich and important ancient Thassos was.

Local tip: Do the Agora and the museum as one visit (they're adjacent). The giant kouros is the must-see piece.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Sanctuary remains (Herakles, Poseidon, Dionysos, Artemis…)

~30–35 min · around Limenas

Scattered through and around the ancient city are the foundations of several sanctuaries — to Herakles (the city's protector), Poseidon, Dionysos, Artemis and others — reflecting the gods the Thasians honoured. They're mostly low ruins and foundations, but they fill in the religious life of the ancient town as you walk the site.

Local tip: These reward a slow wander with a site map rather than a quick look — combine with the wall walk.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Ancient marble quarries

varies · Aliki (south) + areas near Limenas

Thassos was famous in antiquity for its brilliant white marble, exported across the ancient world. You can still see the ancient quarries where it was cut — most strikingly at Aliki, where worked quarry faces and half-cut blocks run right down into (and under) the sea. Quarrying shaped the island's wealth for over two thousand years.

Local tip: The Aliki quarry is the easiest and most dramatic to visit and combines with the beach and basilica there — see below.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Aliki — double basilica & ancient quarry

~45–55 min · south-east peninsula

On the little Aliki peninsula, above the twin coves, sit the ruins of two adjoining early-Christian basilicas (the 'double basilica') on a headland with marble columns against the sea, plus the ancient marble quarry that scored the rock and shoreline. One of the most beautiful archaeological-plus-swimming spots on the island — antiquity, sea and snorkelling in one place.

Local tip: Walk the headland loop to the basilica columns and the quarry, then swim in the sheltered cove. Bring a snorkel to see worked marble underwater. Magical at golden hour.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Monastery of Archangel Michael (Moni Archangelou)

~45–55 min · south-east coast, near Alyki/Astris road · clifftop

A working convent dramatically perched on the cliffs high above the sea on the south-east coast, gleaming white against the blue. It guards a relic (a nail of the Crucifixion) and a spring of holy water, and the views down the cliffs are spectacular. The most visited monastery on Thassos.

Local tip: Dress modestly (shoulders and knees covered — wraps are usually provided at the entrance). Go in the cooler part of the day, fill a bottle at the holy spring, and take in the cliff views. Easy to combine with the south/Aliki loop.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Kastro (mountain village & old fortress refuge)

~50–60 min (last stretch rough road) · high interior

Historically a fortified mountain refuge where islanders sheltered from pirate raids — today an atmospheric, largely abandoned stone village high in the mountains, with a church and old houses, reached by a long mountain road. It matters as living evidence of how Thasians survived the dangerous centuries by hiding inland.

Local tip: Treat it as a half-day mountain expedition with Theologos and Maries; check road conditions first and carry water and fuel.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

🍽️ Tavernas & restaurants

Eating is one of the great pleasures of Thassos, and it splits neatly by geography: fresh fish and seafood at the little harbour tavernas of the west coast (right where you're based), and grilled meat, goat and lamb up in the mountain villages. The island also does its own honey, olive oil, wine and tsipouro. To eat like a local, skip the printed menu and ask «Τι έχει σήμερα;» ('what's good today?') — and in a fish taverna, ask to see the catch and have it priced by the kilo before it's cooked.

West-coast fish tavernas (Skala Sotiros & Skala Kallirachis)

~0–15 min · west coast · fresh fish & seafood

Your home advantage. The small seafront tavernas in Skala Sotiros and, just down the coast, Skala Kallirachis serve the fishing boats' own catch a few metres from where it's landed — grilled fish, fried small fish (marides, gavros), octopus on the grill, calamari, mussels, and Greek salad with local olive oil. Quiet, family-run, sea right there, sunset included.

Local tip: Ask which fish came in that day and have it weighed and priced before cooking — that's how locals avoid surprises. Grilled fresh fish + horta (wild greens) + a carafe of local white = the perfect west-coast dinner.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Mountain tavernas — Sotiras, Panagia, Theologos

~10–40 min · upper villages · grilled meat & goat

For meat, go up. Theologos is the island's temple of spit-roasted lamb and goat (kid) — slow-cooked for hours over coals. Panagia does excellent grilled lamb and traditional oven dishes in its plane-shaded square. Your own Sotiras serves grills with a sunset view. Expect chops, kontosouvli, kokkinisto goat, country sausages, oven-baked dishes, and local wine straight from the barrel.

Local tip: In Theologos go for the spit-roast goat/lamb specifically — it's what the village is famous for. Order a few mezedes to share and the house wine; book ahead on busy August nights.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Limenas & Limenaria options

~25–35 min · north & south towns · wide choice

The two towns give you range. Limenas (Thassos Town) has a string of tavernas and ouzeri around the picturesque old fishing harbour (Palio Limani) — atmospheric for seafood with the boats bobbing in front of you. Limenaria has plenty of waterfront tavernas and tsipouradika. Both also have more modern/international options if you want a change from village cooking.

Local tip: In Limenas, eat at the old harbour rather than the main strip for atmosphere. Pair with the archaeology (Limenas) or the Palataki and seafront stroll (Limenaria).

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

☕ Coffee shops & cafés

Greek café culture runs deep on Thassos, in two forms. The traditional kafeneio — the old village coffee house on the square, where men play tavli (backgammon), the coffee is a proper ellinikos (Greek coffee) and time slows right down — and the modern seafront kafeteria serving freddo espresso and freddo cappuccino (iced, the Greek summer staple), frappé, and fresh juices. You'll find both within a short drive of Casa Valesa.

Village kafeneia (Sotiras, Theologos, Panagia, the Prinos villages)

~10–40 min · upper villages · traditional

For the real thing, take a coffee in a plane-shaded village square. A kafeneio in Sotiras, Theologos, Panagia or Megalos Prinos (Kazaviti) gives you a proper Greek coffee or a glass of cold spring water, shade, and the unhurried rhythm of village life — often paired with a spoon-sweet (glyko tou koutaliou) or local honey.

Local tip: Order an <em>ellinikos metrios</em> (medium-sweet Greek coffee), sit under the plane tree, and don't rush — that's the whole point. Cash is handy in the smallest villages.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Seafront cafés (Skala Sotiros & the west-coast skales)

~0–15 min · west coast · freddo & juice

Your morning-coffee and sunset-drink spots are on your own coast: simple seafront cafés in Skala Sotiros and the neighbouring ports where you can have a freddo espresso, a frappé or a fresh juice with your feet almost in the water, quietly, even in high season.

Local tip: A freddo espresso in the morning and a sunset coffee or beer in the evening, both walkable — one of the joys of basing yourself on the quiet coast.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Limenas & Limenaria café scene

~25–35 min · towns · busier, modern

When you want buzz and choice, the towns deliver. Limenas has a lively café scene around the harbour and marina — busy, social, good for people-watching and a long iced coffee. Limenaria's waterfront has plenty of cafés too. Both are where younger crowds and families pass the afternoon.

Local tip: Combine town café time with a museum/town-walk morning (Limenas) or a seafront stroll (Limenaria).

🌙 Nightlife

Be honest with yourself about Thassos: it's a low-key, relaxed island, not a party destination. Nightlife means beach bars, seafront cocktail bars, late tavernas and the odd club in the resorts — not big clubbing. For many guests that's exactly the appeal, and the quiet west coast (your base) is the calmest of all: evenings here are about a long dinner and a sunset drink, not a dance floor. When you do want a night out, you drive to the resorts.

Limenas waterfront

~30–35 min · north · bars & cocktails

The capital has the island's most reliable evening scene: cocktail bars and music bars along the waterfront and around the old harbour, busy in season and lasting later than the villages. The best all-round option for a proper night out with atmosphere.

Local tip: Park once and walk the harbour front — bars are clustered, so you can drift between them.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Potos & Golden Beach beach bars

~30–40 min · south & east · younger crowd

Potos has the liveliest south-coast strip of bars and a younger holiday crowd; Golden Beach and Paradise Beach have beach bars that run from afternoon cocktails into the evening with music. This is where the under-30s tend to head.

Local tip: Golden Beach / Paradise beach bars are great in the late afternoon — sunset drinks with your feet in the sand before dinner.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Limenaria

~25–30 min · south-west · relaxed seafront bars

The south town has a steady, relaxed seafront bar scene — cafés that turn into bars, a few music spots — busier than the west coast but more low-key than Limenas or Potos. A pleasant easy evening close to home.

Local tip: A good middle-ground night out — closer than Potos, livelier than your own coast.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

The quiet west coast (your base)

~0–15 min · west · deliberately calm

Around Skala Sotiros and the Prinos/Kallirachis ports, 'nightlife' is a candle-lit table at a fish taverna, the sound of the sea, and a digestif of tsipouro after dinner. That calm is the selling point — you sleep to silence, not a bass line.

Local tip: Make a feature of it: late seafront dinner, a glass of tsipouro, then the stars. Drive to Limenas/Potos only when you actively want noise.

🫒 Agrotourism & alternative tourism

Thassos is a farming and fishing island as much as a beach one, and αγροτουρισμός / εναλλακτικός τουρισμός (agrotourism / alternative tourism) is one of the most rewarding ways to experience it — especially from the rural west coast. Olive groves, honey, wine and tsipouro, mountain villages and traditional festivals (panigiria) all sit a short drive from Casa Valesa. This is the slow, authentic Thassos: meet the producers, taste at source, and time a visit to a village feast if you can.

Olive groves & olive-oil tasting

~0–30 min · west & south · year-round (harvest Oct–Dec)

Olives are everywhere on Thassos, and the west coast around Prinos and Kallirachi is prime olive country. The island's small-fruited throumba olives and its green-gold olive oil are a point of local pride. Several producers and small mills offer tastings, and you can buy oil, table olives and olive products direct.

Local tip: Buy your cooking oil and table olives direct from a local producer or the village co-op — far better and cheaper than the supermarket. Olive-grove walks near Skala Sotiros are lovely in the soft evening light.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Thassos honey & beekeeping

varies · mountain & forest areas · spring–autumn

Thassos honey — especially pine and thyme honey from its forests — is famous across Greece and one of the island's signature products. Roadside stalls and village shops sell it straight from local beekeepers, and you'll see hives in the pine woods as you drive the mountain roads.

Local tip: Pine honey (meli peukou) is the local hero — buy a jar direct from a producer. Pair it with local thick yoghurt and walnuts for the classic Greek dessert.

🧭 Directions from Casa Valesa

Wine & tsipouro / local producers

~0–30 min · west villages · year-round

The west-coast villages have a long tradition of small-scale wine and tsipouro (the Greek grappa) making, often from the barrel. Combined with the honey, oil, herbs and goat's cheese, it's the makings of a proper local larder — much of it sold informally village-to-visitor.

Local tip: Ask your taverna for the <em>krasi chyma</em> (barrel wine) and try the local tsipouro as a digestif. Some village tavernas sell their own.

Festivals & panigiria (saint's-day feasts)

seasonal · villages island-wide · mainly summer

Through the summer the villages hold panigiria — open-air feasts on their patron saint's day with food, barrel wine, and live music and traditional dancing in the square late into the night. They're warm, communal and very welcoming to visitors. The biggest cluster around mid-August (the Dekapentavgoustos / 15 August feast of the Panagia) — Panagia village is especially lively then.

Local tip: Ask locally which village has its panigiri while you're here and go — it's the single most authentic night you can have on the island. Bring cash and an appetite.

⛵ Boating & sailing

Seen from the water, Thassos is a different island — coves and sea caves you can't reach by road, the white-marble shallows of the north, and the calm, sheltered water of the west coast that's ideal for gentle boating. You can join an organised day cruise from the main ports, or rent a small boat yourself and explore at your own pace.

Day cruises & round-the-island boat trips

departs Limenas / Limenaria / Potos · ~30–35 min to ports

The classic option: a full- or half-day cruise that circles all or part of the island, stopping to swim and snorkel in turquoise bays, reaching coves like Marble Beach by sea, and usually serving lunch and drinks on board. Choices range from big family/party boats and 'pirate ship' cruises to smaller, calmer trips.

Local tip: A boat day is the easiest, nicest way to reach Marble Beach and the wild north shore without the rough road. Book a day or two ahead in peak season and pick a smaller boat if you want calm over party.

🎣 Fishing

Thassos still has a real, working fishing culture — small caïques going out from every harbour and bringing the catch back to the seafront tavernas. On the quiet west coast that culture is right outside your door, and there are several ways to get involved, from a quiet line off the rocks to a proper boat trip.

Local fishing-boat culture (the caïques)

~0–15 min · west-coast harbours · early morning & dusk

The little ports — Skala Sotiros, Skala Prinos, Skala Kallirachis, the old harbour at Limenas — are home to traditional wooden fishing boats. Watching them come in and the day's catch being landed and sold to the tavernas is part of the island's everyday rhythm, especially at dawn and dusk.

Local tip: Wander down to your local harbour in the early morning to see (and sometimes buy) the catch as it lands — and to spot which taverna got the best fish for that night.

🥾 Walking & hiking paths

Thassos is wonderful walking country — pine and chestnut forest, olive groves, springs, old mule paths between the villages, and a marked European long-distance trail crossing the island. Walks range from gentle olive-grove strolls near Skala Sotiros to proper mountain hikes. Always carry water, wear real shoes, start early in summer, and tell someone your route on the remoter trails.

The E6 European long-distance path / island trails

various trailheads · all over the island

The European E6 long-distance path runs across Thassos, and there's a wider network of signed walking trails linking the mountain villages along old footpaths through forest and past springs. They range from easy village-to-village links to longer mountain sections — a great way to see the green interior on foot.

Local tip: Pick a marked section between two villages rather than committing to a whole crossing; check signage locally and carry plenty of water — there's little shade on the open stretches.

📜 A short history of Thassos

Antiquity — a Parian colony of gold and marble. Thassos was colonised around the early 7th century BC by settlers from the Cycladic island of Paros — among them, by tradition, the father of the poet Archilochos, who wrote vividly about the new island. The colonists quickly grew rich from two things: the gold and silver mines of the island and the nearby Thracian mainland (the Pangaion region), and the island's superb white marble. Ancient Thassos became a powerful, independent city-state, minting its own coinage, fortifying its capital with magnificent marble walls and gates, and trading its famous wine and marble across the Mediterranean. Both Herodotus and Thucydides write about Thassos: Herodotus describes its rich mines and the huge revenues they brought, and Thucydides records the island's revolt from the Athenian-led Delian League around 465 BC, which Athens crushed after a long siege — a turning point that cost Thassos its walls, fleet and mainland mines.

The white marble. Thassian marble — brilliantly white, fine-grained and faintly sparkling — was prized throughout the ancient world and quarried here for more than two millennia. You can still see the ancient quarries, most dramatically at Aliki on the south-east coast, where cut blocks run down into the sea. Marble (and the gold that first made the island wealthy) is the thread running through the whole of Thassos's story, and the reason its seabed and beaches shine so white today.

Roman, Byzantine and Ottoman centuries. Under Rome, Thassos kept exporting marble and remained a notable centre. In the Byzantine era it became Christian — the early-Christian basilicas at Aliki and elsewhere date from this time — but, like all the Aegean, it suffered repeated pirate raids, which is why islanders moved their main settlements inland and uphill (Theologos, Panagia, Kastro) and away from the exposed coast. After a period under the Genoese, Thassos fell under Ottoman rule. For much of the 19th century it was, unusually, governed on behalf of the Ottoman viceroys of Egypt (the family of Muhammad Ali, who was born in nearby Kavala), enjoying a degree of autonomy. Theologos served as the island's capital through much of this period. Thassos was finally united with Greece during the Balkan Wars, in 1912.

The mining era and modern tourism. The island's old mineral wealth had a final chapter in the late 19th and 20th centuries, when foreign companies — notably the German industrialist Speidel and later the Belgian Vieille Montagne company — mined zinc, iron and other ores around Limenaria and the western/southern hills (including the Sotiras area above Skala Sotiros). The legacy is still visible: the grand Palataki mining-company mansion above Limenaria harbour, old galleries and loading installations, and the mining heritage of the mountain villages like Sotiras. When mining wound down, Thassos turned to what it had all along — its forests, beaches, marble-clear water and traditional villages — and became one of northern Greece's favourite holiday islands, hugely popular with visitors from the Balkans and beyond, while the quiet west coast (your side) has kept much of its older, slower character.

🗺️ Ready-made day trips from Skala Sotiros

Round-the-island by car (the classic ~100 km loop)

The single best orientation day. The coast road circles the whole island (about 95–100 km) and from Skala Sotiros you can go either way; clockwise works nicely. Morning: head north to Limenas / Thassos Town (~30–35 min) — Agora, Archaeological Museum, walk up to the ancient theatre, coffee at the old harbour. Continue along the north coast past the Marble Beach / Saliara turn-off (detour if you have a sturdy car or skip it for a boat/jeep day), down the green east coast to Golden Beach for a swim and lunch, with an inland hop up to Panagia (springs, plane trees). Afternoon: carry on south past Kinira and Aliki (stop for the double basilica, ancient quarry and a swim), round the southern tip via the Archangel Michael monastery and Astris/Giola, to Potos and Limenaria on the south-west. Evening: follow the quiet west coast home through the Prinos villages back to Skala Sotiros — arriving in time for sunset and dinner on your own coast. Tip: pick just 4–5 stops rather than trying to do everything; fill the tank, and carry water and swim gear all day.

Jeep / 4x4 safari to Marble Beach (and the wild north)

Marble Beach (Saliara) and a few northern coves are reached by rough, unpaved tracks that are hard on a small hire car. The easy, stress-free answer is an organised jeep / 4x4 safari (booked in Limenas, Skala Prinos or the resorts), which handles the bad roads and usually strings together Marble Beach, a viewpoint or two, a mountain village and sometimes a waterfall, with a swim stop. If you'd rather drive yourself, go to Marble Beach early, take the track slowly, and turn back if it's too rough or wet. Either way: early start, water shoes, and don't leave it for the middle of a busy August day.

Mountain-villages loop (Sotiras → Maries → Theologos → Panagia)

A half- or full-day inland escape into the green, traditional heart of the island — and the best antidote to a beach day. From Skala Sotiros climb first to your own Sotiras village (10–15 min) for the views and the old mining feel, then cut across the interior to peaceful Maries (and, if you have a good car and time, push on toward Kastro and the waterfall/nature country). Drop down to Theologos, the old capital, for a long lunch of spit-roasted goat or lamb and a wander down its stone main street. Finish at Panagia with its springs, plane-shaded square and traditional sweets. Cool, shaded, slow — and full of real village life.

Sunset dinner up the mountain

The simplest, most repeatable Casa Valesa ritual. In the late afternoon drive 10–15 minutes up to Sotiras village above Skala Sotiros, grab a taverna terrace facing west, and watch the sun drop into the sea over your own coast while you eat grilled meat and drink the local wine. For variety, the terraces of Megalos/Mikros Prinos (Kazaviti) or a seafront table back down in Skala Sotiros or Skala Kallirachis give the same sunset from a different angle. No booking needed most nights, but worth a call in August.

Boat day — round-the-island by sea

Trade the car for the water. Day cruises leave from Limenas, Limenaria and Potos and circle all or part of the island, reaching coves and sea caves you can't easily get to by road, with swim and snorkel stops in turquoise water and usually lunch on board. Some are bigger party/family boats, others small-group or 'pirate-ship' style. From the calm west coast you can also rent a small boat (no licence required for low-power engines) from a nearby harbour and potter between the quiet Prinos/Kallirachis coves yourself. A great alternative way to reach Marble Beach and the wild north shore.

Giola + Aliki south loop (rock pool, ancient quarry, monastery)

A focused south-coast day combining the island's most spectacular natural and historic spots. Drive ~50–60 min to the Giola natural rock lagoon and go early (park up top, walk down, swim in the rock pool before the crowds and midday sun). Then loop to Aliki for the twin coves, the half-submerged ancient marble quarry and the double basilica on the headland — swim and snorkel here. Add the clifftop Monastery of Archangel Michael for the views and holy spring (dress modestly), and finish with lunch at a south-coast taverna in Astris or Potos before the drive home up the quiet west coast.
