How to Get to Thassos from Serbia by Car

Getting There / Road Trip · 9 min read

Serbia to Thassos is the classic Balkan summer drive — straight down the corridor through North Macedonia, into Greece past Thessaloniki, and along the coast to a short ferry. It's very doable, but it's long enough that most families break it overnight, and there are a few road-charge details to get right so nothing slows you down.

Here's the full, honest guide to driving Thassos from Serbia by car — the route via Thessaloniki, Kavala and Keramoti, realistic timing, the vignettes and tolls you'll pay, how the ferry works, and the easy parking waiting on the west coast.

Key takeaways

  • Route: Serbia → North Macedonia → Thessaloniki → Kavala → Keramoti → ferry to Thassos.
  • It's a long drive — break it with an overnight in or near Thessaloniki.
  • Two borders, one non-EU (Serbia–North Macedonia); carry a green card covering all three countries.
  • Budget for North Macedonian and Greek toll booths; the standard route avoids Bulgaria.
  • Keramoti → Limenas ferry is ~40–45 min; Skala Sotiros offers the easiest parking on arrival.

The route: via Thessaloniki to Keramoti

The standard drive runs: Serbia → North Macedonia → Greek border (Evzoni) → Thessaloniki → Kavala → Keramoti → ferry to Thassos.

From Belgrade or Niš you head south on the corridor through North Macedonia, cross into Greece, skirt Thessaloniki, then follow the Egnatia motorway east toward Kavala and turn off to the small port of Keramoti. The final approach to Keramoti is flat and easy — not mountain driving, a welcome end to a long day.

From Keramoti the short, frequent ferry takes you across to Limenas (Thassos Town); it's the closest port for drivers and runs most often. Sailings and tickets are covered in the Thassos ferry guide.

Timing: why Thessaloniki is the natural overnight

Serbia to Thassos is a long drive — comfortably more than a single relaxed day for most families, especially with children. The smart move is to break it around Thessaloniki:

  • Day 1: Serbia → through North Macedonia → into Greece → overnight in or near Thessaloniki. This puts the hardest border-and-corridor driving behind you.
  • Day 2: a short, easy run from Thessaloniki east to Kavala and Keramoti (a couple of hours), arriving fresh for the ferry.

Thessaloniki is the natural stopover because it's roughly where the long first leg ends and the easy coastal leg begins, and it has plenty of hotels with parking. If you'd rather push through in one very long day from southern Serbia, it's possible — but the overnight makes the whole trip calmer and gets you to the ferry without racing the clock.

Borders: North Macedonia and Greece

You cross two borders, and one of them is a non-EU crossing, so allow time:

  • Serbia → North Macedonia: a non-Schengen border — usually fine, but it can queue in peak summer. Have documents ready.
  • North Macedonia → Greece (Evzoni / Bogorodica): the crossing into the EU. Again straightforward but a potential summer bottleneck on weekend mornings.

Carry passports/IDs, vehicle registration and a valid green card/insurance that covers Serbia, North Macedonia and Greece — check your green card lists all three. An early start helps you hit the borders before the crowds.

Vignettes and tolls along the way

This route touches three road-charge regimes — plan for each:

  • North Macedonia: the main corridor (the A1 toward Greece) uses per-section toll booths — keep some euro/denar cash for these.
  • Greece — per-section tolls (διόδια): on the motorway past Thessaloniki and east toward Kavala you pay at staffed booths. Keep euro cash and coins handy; cards work but cash is fastest in a queue.

If your routing or a detour takes you through any Bulgarian motorway, note Bulgaria uses an electronic vignette — but the standard Serbia route via Thessaloniki avoids Bulgaria entirely. Budget mainly for the North Macedonian and Greek tolls, which are modest for a family holiday.

Fuel and rest stops

Plan fuel around the borders and the overnight:

  • Top up before long border-area stretches so you're not searching for a station at a bad moment.
  • The corridor through North Macedonia and the Greek motorway both have good service stations for coffee, food and a leg-stretch.
  • If you overnight in Thessaloniki, fill the tank there so day two is a clean run to the ferry.
  • Carry water and snacks — the final Greek stretch and the ferry marshalling area offer little shade in high summer.

The ferry and an easy arrival

At Keramoti you drive into the port, follow the lanes and buy your car ticket at the booth (vehicle + passengers — keep cash handy). The Keramoti → Limenas crossing is about 40–45 minutes and frequent, so waits are short outside the August peak. In high summer, aim for an earlier or later sailing rather than the busiest midday boats to skip the car queue.

Off the boat at Limenas, it's a calm 20–25 minute coast drive to the west-coast villages. And after two days of driving, the welcome detail is this: Skala Sotiros is one of the easiest villages on the island to park — quiet streets, free spaces, room for a loaded car by your door. You arrive, park once, and the road trip is over.

Where to recover and base yourself: the west coast

After a Serbia-to-Greece drive you want calm, not chaos — and the west coast of Thassos delivers exactly that. The afternoon water stays gentle here while the meltemi wind roughens the east and north, and the sun sets straight into the sea rather than behind a mountain — the kind of evening that makes the drive feel worth it.

Skala Sotiros is the pick: easy parking, a short flat walk to its own pebble-and-sand shore, and a sensible drive to every beach and sight on the island. Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to drive to Thassos from Serbia?

It's a long drive that most families split over two days, with a natural overnight in or near Thessaloniki. Day two from Thessaloniki to the Keramoti ferry is only a couple of hours, plus the ~40–45 minute crossing and a 20–25 minute coast drive.

Which route do I take to Thassos from Serbia?

Through North Macedonia into Greece, past Thessaloniki, then east on the Egnatia motorway to Kavala and Keramoti, where you take the ferry to Limenas. This route avoids Bulgaria entirely.

What documents do I need driving from Serbia to Thassos?

Passports/IDs, vehicle registration and a valid green card/insurance that covers Serbia, North Macedonia and Greece. You cross two borders — Serbia–North Macedonia (non-EU) and North Macedonia–Greece.

What tolls and vignettes do I pay from Serbia to Thassos?

Per-section toll booths in North Macedonia and Greece — keep cash handy. The standard Thessaloniki route avoids Bulgaria, so you don't need a Bulgarian e-vignette unless you detour through Bulgarian motorways.

Which ferry do I take and is parking easy when I arrive?

Take Keramoti → Limenas (about 40–45 minutes, frequent). On the west coast, Skala Sotiros is one of the easiest villages to park — quiet streets and free spaces with room for a loaded car, unlike Limenas in August.
