How to Get to Thassos from Romania by Car

Getting There / Road Trip · 10 min read

Thassos is one of the closest proper-Greek-island holidays a Romanian family can drive to — no flights, no luggage limits, the whole boot packed with whatever you want. But it's a long single day on the road, crossing two countries, and the difference between an easy trip and a frazzled one is in the planning: the right border, the vignette, the tolls, and catching the ferry without a queue.

This is the honest, end-to-end guide to driving Thassos from Romania by car — the full route via Ruse/Giurgiu, Sofia and Keramoti, realistic timing, what you pay in Bulgaria and Greece, and how the last flat stretch leads straight onto the boat.

Key takeaways

  • Route: Romania → Giurgiu/Ruse → Sofia → Drama/Kavala → Keramoti → ferry to Thassos.
  • Plan a long single day from Bucharest; overnight near Sofia if you start further north.
  • Buy a Bulgarian electronic vignette before the motorways; pay Greek tolls at the booths.
  • Fuel is cheaper in Bulgaria — fill up before the Greek border.
  • The last stretch to Keramoti is flat and easy; take an early/late ferry to skip the August car queue.

The route in one line

The classic drive is: Romania → Ruse/Giurgiu border → Sofia → Greek border → Drama / Kavala → Keramoti → ferry → Thassos.

You cross the Danube from Giurgiu into Ruse, run south through Bulgaria past Sofia, drop down to the Greek border, then follow the motorway east toward Kavala and turn off to the small port of Keramoti. The very last stretch into Keramoti is flat and easy — not mountain driving — which is a relief at the end of a long day.

From Keramoti you take the short, frequent ferry to Limenas (Thassos Town), then drive 20–25 minutes along the coast to the calm west-coast villages. Why Keramoti and not Kavala? It's the closest port to the Bulgarian approach and runs the most often — full reasoning in our Thassos ferry guide.

Distance and timing: one long day or an overnight?

Depending on where in Romania you start, you're looking at a serious day behind the wheel:

  • From Bucharest: roughly a long single day — plan a dawn start, drive through, and aim to be at Keramoti for a late-afternoon or early-evening ferry.
  • From Transylvania / the north (Cluj, Brașov, Sibiu): add several hours — most families break it with an overnight, commonly around Sofia or just inside Bulgaria, then do a relaxed half-day to the coast and the ferry.

Honest advice: if your total driving day creeps past 9–10 hours with kids in the car, split it. An overnight near Sofia turns a punishing marathon into two pleasant drives, and you arrive at the ferry fresh rather than fried. Either way, build in a buffer so you're not racing the last boat — Keramoti runs often, but the August car queue is real (more below).

Borders: Giurgiu–Ruse and the Greek crossing

You cross two borders. Neither is dramatic, but know what to expect:

  • Giurgiu → Ruse (RO→BG): over the Danube bridge. There's a bridge toll here, paid at the crossing. Queues can build in peak summer, especially weekend mornings — an early start helps.
  • Bulgaria → Greece: a straightforward EU internal crossing (Promachonas or the Kulata–Promachonas corridor depending on your line). Usually quick; have IDs/passports ready for spot checks.

Carry your green card / insurance documents, vehicle registration and ID for everyone. Both Bulgaria and Greece are in the EU, so it's all simple paperwork — just have it in the glovebox, not the bottom of a bag.

Bulgarian vignette and Greek tolls

Two separate road-charge systems on this trip — budget for both:

  • Bulgaria — vignette (toll sticker): Bulgaria uses an electronic vignette (no physical sticker needed now — it's tied to your plate). Buy it before you use the motorways: online, at the border area, or at petrol stations. A weekend or short-period vignette is cheap and is the standard buy for a holiday transit. Driving without one risks a fine.
  • Greece — per-section tolls (διόδια): Greece charges tolls at staffed booths on the Egnatia and connecting motorways as you head east toward Kavala. You pay cash or card at each booth — keep some euro coins and small notes handy so you're quick through the lane.

Neither is expensive in the scheme of a family holiday, but forgetting the Bulgarian vignette is the classic Romanian-road-trip mistake — sort it as you enter Bulgaria.

Fuel, food and rest stops

Fuel is generally cheaper in Bulgaria than in either Romania or Greece, so it's smart to fill up in Bulgaria before the Greek border. Practical tips:

  • Top up the tank near Sofia or on the run south so you cross into Greece full.
  • Bulgarian motorway stations (the big chains on the Sofia–Kulata road) have clean facilities and decent food — a good lunch stop.
  • Carry water and snacks for the car; the final Greek stretch and the ferry queue have limited shade.
  • If you're overnighting, a hotel with parking near Sofia keeps the second day short and the car secure.

The easy last stretch — and onto the ferry

Here's the good news after a big day: the final approach is gentle. Once you're past the Greek border and on the motorway toward Kavala, the drive to Keramoti is flat and straightforward — no hairpins, no mountain passes with a loaded car. You roll into the port area, follow the lanes, and buy your car ticket at the booth (vehicle + passengers, cash handy).

The Keramoti → Limenas crossing is about 40–45 minutes and runs frequently. In peak August the only catch is the car queue — so aim for an earlier or later sailing rather than the busy midday boats, and you'll often drive straight on. Full ticket-and-queue detail is in the ferry guide.

Off the boat at Limenas, it's a calm 20–25 minute coast drive to the west-coast villages — and the holiday has finally begun.

Coming from Bulgaria instead, or comparing routes

If part of your family or your route actually starts in or runs through Bulgaria, the Sofia → Drama/Kavala → Keramoti leg is an easy one-day drive on its own — we break that down separately in how to get to Thassos from Bulgaria by car. The Romanian trip is essentially the Bulgarian trip plus the Danube crossing and a few extra hours, so everything in that guide about tolls, fuel and the ferry applies to you too.

Arriving with a full boot — where to park and stay

After 800-plus kilometres and a ferry, the last thing you want is to circle Limenas hunting for a parking space. This is exactly why Romanian road-trippers lean toward the west coast: Skala Sotiros is one of the easiest villages on the island to park — quiet streets, free spaces, room for a loaded car right by your door. You arrive, you park once, and the boot finally gets unpacked in peace.

It's also where the afternoon water stays calmest and the sun sets straight into the sea — a gentle, family-friendly side of Thassos to come home to each evening. Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to drive to Thassos from Romania?

From Bucharest it's a long single day to the Keramoti ferry; from northern Romania (Cluj, Brașov) most families add an overnight near Sofia. Add about 40–45 minutes for the ferry to Limenas plus a 20–25 minute coast drive to the west-coast villages.

Which ferry do I take to Thassos when driving from Romania?

Keramoti → Limenas. It's the closest port to the Bulgarian/Romanian approach and the most frequent crossing (about 40–45 minutes). Buy your car ticket at the port booth.

Do I need a vignette to drive through Bulgaria to Thassos?

Yes. Bulgaria requires an electronic vignette linked to your plate — buy it before using the motorways (online, at the border or at petrol stations). In Greece you then pay per-section tolls at staffed booths.

Where should I fill up with fuel on the way to Thassos?

Fuel is generally cheaper in Bulgaria than in Romania or Greece, so fill up in Bulgaria — ideally near Sofia or on the run south — so you cross into Greece with a full tank.

Is the drive to Thassos difficult or mountainous?

No. The final approach to Keramoti is flat and easy, not mountain driving. The route is mostly motorway, with two simple EU borders. The only seasonal hassle is the August car queue at the ferry, which an early or late sailing avoids.
